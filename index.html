Research Background



I was born and raised in a remote village (popularly called Ukhubari Tapu) of Syangja district in Nepal, the mountainous country in south asia. Although not acquainted with any means of science and technology during my school years (not even with the electricity and motor vehicles), I was very fond of starring at the majestic view of the night sky as seen from my home town, undimmed by the city lights. I had have a curious mind to know the secret of the astronomical objects and the distances these objects were probing. It was only after I got to Kathmandu (the capital city of Nepal) to start my higher education that I discovered about the science that deals with the study of the Universe was called Astrophysics.

I did my Bachelor and Masters degrees in Physics from Tribhuvan University, Nepal. My masters thesis was focussed on General Relativity and Cosmology in which I studied the effect of Kerr parameter on the rotation curve of galaxies, under the supervision of Prof. Udayraj Khanal, a renowned theorist and a science motivator in Nepal. Then, I moved to University of South Carolina, Columbia, USA to pursue my PhD in Physics. During my PhD, I worked under the supervision of Prof. Varsha P. Kulkarni to study the formation and evolution of galaxies at high-redshift using the quasar absorption line systems. My PhD thesis was about the cosmic metal evolution during the first ~1 billion years after the big bang using gas-rich quasar absorbers. Currently, I am working as a postdoctoral researcher at Instituto de Física, Pontificia Universidad Católica de Valparaíso (PUCV), Chile, funded by ESO-Chile Joint Committee grant to study the circum-galactic medium (CGM) at high-redshift through the gravitational-arc tomography technique, under the supervision of Prof. Nicolas Tejos.

My Research

My research is mainly focussed on the absorption line systems and their contribution to the galaxy formation and evolution in the distant universe. I use quasars as the background source of light to probe the foreground galaxies. The photons that left the quasars several billion years back in time, travel through the circum-galactic medium(CGM) of the intervening galaxies and the properties of the galaxy environments get imprinted in the spectra of the background quasars. I'm particularly interested in the sub-DLAs/DLAs absorbers which dominate the neutral gas mass density of the universe and are the gas reservoirs for star formation at high-redshift.

I have used some of the world's largest telescopes such as Keck Telescope in Hawaii, Large Binocular Telescope (LBT) in Arizona, Very Large Telescope (VLT) and Magellan Telescopes in Chile for the spectroscopic observation of the quasars both at the high and medium resolution and in the optical as well as NIR wavelengths. Following are some highlights of the research I'm involved in.

Dust, metals, and gas kinematics in high-z DLAs

Observations of DLAs at 0 < z < 4.5 indicate that their metallicity decreases at a modest rate of about 0.2 dex per unit redshift. However, some studies have reported a sudden drop in DLA metallicity at z > 4.7 which, if real, may suggest sudden changes in gas enrichment processes. This relatively small z > 4.7 sample is based almost entirely on the elements Si and Fe which show significant depletion on dust grains in the ISM of the Milky Way (MW). Indeed, Si and Fe are observed widely to be depleted in DLAs as well. Given the potentially interesting implications of a sudden drop in metallicity at z ~ 5 and the conflict such a drop would produce with existing chemical evolution models, it is especially important to check with a larger sample using undepleted elements (e.g. S, O). The measurements at z > 4.5 are highly challenging because of the severe blending of metal lines with the dense Lyman-alpha forest, difficulty in continuum fitting, and the low S/N reached in the spectra of the high-z quasars. We are expanding the measurements of S and/or O in sub-DLAs and DLAs at z > 4.5. After combining a handful of dust-free metallicity measurements from the literature with our new measurements at z > 4.5, we observed the metallicity value to be in excellent agreement with the prediction inferred from lower redshift DLAs (see Fig. 1 (Left)). This supports the interpretation that the metallicity evolution is smooth at z ∼ 5, rather than declining suddenly at z > 4.7. The primary reason our finding differs from that of previous studies appears to lie in the fact that we are focusing on elements that do not deplete on to dust grains. Furthermore, these measurements indicate that the metallicity evolution trends for the DLAs and sub-DLAs may be similar at this early epoch.

Moreover, a significant fraction of the z∼5 absorbers show evidence of depletion of elements onto dust grains, e.g. low [Si/O] or [Fe/O]. In a substantial fraction of absorbers at z > 4.5, the extent of dust depletion, as judged by the parameter F⋆ (Jenkins 2009), appears to be at least as significant as (and in some cases stronger than) the typical depletion found in lower-redshift absorbers.

The gas kinematics of a galaxy can be understood from measuring the velocity dispersion of its gas. The value of the velocity dispersion may depend on the inflow or outflow of gases within the galaxy and also on rotation in the disk of the galaxy which depends on the galaxy mass. If the velocity dispersion depends primarily on mass, and the role played by gas flows is small, then the velocity dispersion vs. metallicity relation can suggest a relationship between mass and metallicity of absorber host galaxies. In lower redshift DLAs and sub-DLAs, a fairly tight correlation exists between the metallicity and the velocity dispersion of the gas. However, our recent study show a somewhat flatter relation at z∼5 (see Fig. 1 (Right)), possibly suggesting that the z∼5 absorbers may be tracing a different population of galaxies than the lower redshift absorbers .

All of these findings were part of my PhD thesis and were also published in MNRAS in recent years (see Poudel et al. 2018, Poudel et al. 2020).

Nucleosynthesis and Relative Abundances

The first 1 billion years of the cosmic metal evolution are influenced by the nucleosynthetic signatures from the early stars. Measuring abundances during the z ∼ 5 epoch can constrain the nature of the pristine Population III stars and the early generations of metal-poor Population II stars. The initial mass function (IMF) of Population III stars is still a subject of debate. Measurements of relative abundances such as [Si/O] and [C/O] in chemically young systems can put a strong constraint on the mass of the progenitor Population III stars (e.g. Cooke et al. 2017). We examine the relative abundances in the metal poor z ∼ 5 DLAs to study the nucleosynthetic signatures of the early stars (see Poudel et al. 2020).
